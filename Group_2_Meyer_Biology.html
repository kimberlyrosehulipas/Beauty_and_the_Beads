Key A5 – small ‘fern-like’ pteridophytes, terrestrial, growing in soil, not associated with rock outcrops

Sori elongate; leaf blades somewhat dimorphic, the fertile larger and erect, the sterile smaller and prostrate, the larger leaf blades 2-4 (-6.5) cm wide [Asplenium] ASPLENIACEAE

Source: http://www.herbarium.unc.edu/Big%20Key%20F.pdf

Ferns, like all other plants except true mosses and lichens, are vascular plants, which means they have internal tubes for transporting fluids. Ferns, on the other hand, reproduce through spores rather than seeds. As a result, they prefer moist environments and are unlikely to be found in dry, arid climates. Ferns are distinguished from other low-growing species by their pinnate (divided into numerous branches on opposite sides of a common axis) leaves and simple, narrow stems.

Coleus plants are prized for their vibrant foliage, which can be green, yellow, pink, red, rust, or maroon. This popular summer annual plant's new introductions have been chosen for their increased sun and heat tolerance. Coleus foliage's brilliant and widely varied colors make it natural for use as a summer bedding plant and as a color accent. Coleus thrives in containers, brightening shady areas, patios, porches, and garden terraces.

Codiaeum variegatum is an evergreen perennial species that is commonly grown indoors as a houseplant with colorful foliage. It has large leathery leaves that are brightly colored and heavily veined in red, orange, yellow, gold, and green. The flowers are tiny, white, and star-shaped, and they bloom in the summer. This species grows best with some humidity and does well in a bright conservatory, but it should be kept out of direct sunlight - preferring bright, indirect sunlight - and given plenty of warmth.

Red Button Ginger, so named because of its lovely flower head that emerges as a luscious red button, is one of the robust spiral gingers in the Costus family, which contains over 100 species. It is distinguished by spirally arranged leaves that wrap around the graceful and succulent stems. The plant can tolerate light frost for short periods of time, but it will stop blooming and will not look good. If the plant is kept above 50° all of the time, it will most likely continue to bloom. The red cones that emerge from the plant are also edible.

Source: http://www.herbarium.unc.edu/Big%20Key%20F.pdf

The snake plant is a member of the Lily Family and is known by a variety of other common names. Mother-in-tongue Law's and Bowstring-hemp are both very "politically correct." Sansevieria's durability makes it an excellent choice for apartment dwellers, who frequently have limited success with houseplants due to lighting issues. Sansevieria is the most tolerant of all decorative plants, surviving even the most adverse growing conditions. Basically, the plant is extremely difficult to kill. The snake plant is a classic yet adaptable houseplant with sword-like foliage. It is ideal for the forgetful gardener and is regarded as a top air-purifying plant for the indoor environment.
